## Core Principles

Y Combinator's evaluation philosophy rests on a few fundamental beliefs:

1. **Make something people want** - The single most important question. Does this solve a real, urgent problem?
2. **Founders over ideas** - Great founders pivot; mediocre founders execute bad ideas well. Bet on the people.
3. **Growth is the ultimate proof** - Traction speaks louder than projections. What's actually happening, not what could happen?
4. **Simplicity wins** - The best ideas can be explained in one sentence. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it yet.
5. **Urgency and velocity** - How fast are they moving? Speed of iteration separates winners from losers.

## The Five Questions

When evaluating a startup, answer these five questions in order of priority:

### 1. Is the team relentlessly resourceful?
- Do they find a way past every obstacle?
- Can they sell, code, and hustle?
- Do they have founder-market fit? (Why THIS team for THIS problem?)
- Evidence: track record of getting things done with limited resources

### 2. Is this a big market with a real problem?
- How painful is the problem? (Need-to-have vs nice-to-have)
- How many people have this problem?
- Is the market growing or shifting?
- Evidence: customer validation, not market reports

### 3. Do users love it?
- What does retention look like? (DAU/MAU, cohort retention)
- Would users be upset if the product disappeared?
- Is there organic growth (word of mouth, no paid acquisition)?
- Evidence: engagement metrics, customer interviews

### 4. Can this become a monopoly?
- Is there a wedge/defensibility? (Network effects, data moats, switching costs, brand)
- Does the business get stronger as it grows?
- Can they expand the market over time?
- Evidence: competitive dynamics, unit economics improvement at scale

### 5. Will this be worth $1B+?
- Is the market large enough?
- Is the business model viable at scale?
- What are the realistic outcomes across scenarios?
- Evidence: comparable exits, market sizing

## Signal Detection

### Strong positive signals
- Users who won't shut up about the product
- Revenue growing organically week over week
- Founders who've experienced the problem personally
- Clear, unambiguous progress between conversations
- Working prototype with real users, even if ugly
- Founders who can articulate what they've learned from failures

### Red flags
- "We just need X% of a Y billion market" (top-down market sizing)
- No clear answer to "who are your first 10 customers?"
- Founders who talk more about features than problems
- Elegance of presentation over substance of metrics
- Long explanations for why things haven't worked yet
- Unwillingness to pick a beachhead market

## Anti-Patterns

Avoid investing when you see:

1. **Solutions looking for problems** - "We built an AI platform for X" without evidence X needs AI
2. **Stealth mode without reason** - Early-stage startups benefit from feedback, not secrecy
3. **Market size via bottom-up subtraction** - "If we just get 1% of this market..." is not a thesis
4. **Feature, not product** - Could this be a feature of an existing platform?
5. **Slow iteration cycles** - If they haven't shipped or learned in weeks, velocity is too low
6. **First-time founders who don't listen** - Coachability matters more than experience

## Stage-Specific Evaluation

### Pre-product / Idea Stage
Focus: Team quality, problem authenticity, market size
- Is the team capable of building and selling?
- Is the problem real and personal to them?
- What evidence of demand exists (even anecdotal)?

### Pre-revenue / MVP Stage
Focus: Product-market fit signals, team velocity
- Does a working product exist?
- What do early users say and do?
- How fast is iteration happening?

### Post-revenue / Early Growth
Focus: Unit economics, growth rate, market dynamics
- Is revenue growing week over week?
- Are unit economics improving or at least stable?
- What does retention look like across cohorts?
- Is there a clear path to profitability?

### Growth / Series A+
Focus: Scaling metrics, market position, defensibility
- Are growth rates sustainable?
- What's the competitive moat?
- Can the team scale with the business?
- What are the expansion opportunities?
