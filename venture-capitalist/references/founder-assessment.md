## Overview

For early-stage investing, the founding team is the primary thesis. Markets pivot, products change, but the founders persist. This framework evaluates founders through the YC lens: prioritize relentlessness, resourcefulness, and founder-market fit above credentials.

## The YC Founder Profile

### What YC Looks For

1. **Relentlessly resourceful** — The Paul Graham test: "Could you imagine this person working for you, or would you end up working for them?"
2. **Flexible thinker** — Updates beliefs based on evidence. Smart about *what* to change and *what* to persist with.
3. **Missionary, not mercenary** — Building because they care deeply about the problem, not because it's trendy.
4. **Action-biased** — Ships fast, iterates, learns from real user feedback.
5. **Clear communicator** — Can explain the problem and solution simply and compellingly.

### What YC Avoids

1. **Uncoachably stubborn** — Confident but closed-minded
2. **Resume builders** — In it for the title or the exit
3. **Slow shippers** — All talk, no product
4. **Theory-first** — Wants to build the perfect product before showing users
5. **Solo founders without a plan** — Building hard things alone is a red flag (though not disqualifying)

## Assessment Dimensions

### 1. Founder-Market Fit

The single most important dimension. Why is THIS team uniquely suited to solve THIS problem?

**Strong signals:**
- Personal experience with the problem (built something because they needed it)
- Deep domain expertise (years in the industry)
- Unique insight that outsiders would miss
- Technical ability to build the solution themselves

**Weak signals:**
- Read a market report and saw an opportunity
- Building in a space because it's "hot"
- No direct experience with the customer or problem
- Dependent on hiring to build the core product

**Assessment questions:**
- "What's something you believe about this market that most people would disagree with?"
- "Tell me about a time you experienced this problem personally."
- "What have you learned in the last month from talking to users?"

### 2. Execution Velocity

How fast do they move? Speed of iteration is the best predictor of success.

**Strong signals:**
- Shipped a working product (even if ugly)
- Demonstrated rapid iteration based on user feedback
- Previous founding or operating experience with measurable outcomes
- Low time between decision and action

**Weak signals:**
- Weeks pass without visible progress
- "We're going to" > "We did"
- Perfectionism masking fear of feedback
- Endless planning without shipping

**Assessment questions:**
- "What have you shipped in the last two weeks?"
- "Tell me about a time you changed direction based on user feedback."
- "Walk me through your product development cycle."

### 3. Resilience and Grit

Startups are hard. How will this founder respond when things go wrong?

**Strong signals:**
- Previous experience overcoming significant adversity
- Calm under pressure during the evaluation process
- Honest about what's not working
- History of finishing what they started (not just starting things)

**Weak signals:**
- Fragile when challenged or questioned
- Optimism that doesn't acknowledge real risks
- History of abandoning projects when they got hard
- Lack of any evidence of struggle or recovery

**Assessment questions:**
- "Tell me about the hardest thing you've had to overcome."
- "What's something that didn't go as planned and how did you handle it?"
- "When did you realize this was harder than you expected, and what did you do?"

### 4. Intellectual Honesty

Can they think clearly about their business, including its weaknesses?

**Strong signals:**
- Accurately characterizes the state of their business
- Acknowledges what they don't know
- Updates their beliefs based on evidence
- Can articulate why someone might NOT invest

**Weak signals:**
- Overconfident projections without basis
- Dismissing legitimate concerns
- Changing the subject when pressed on weaknesses
- Inability to articulate assumptions underlying their model

**Assessment questions:**
- "What's the strongest argument against investing in your company?"
- "What assumption are you most worried about?"
- "Tell me about something you were wrong about recently."

### 5. Sales Ability

Every founder needs to sell — to customers, employees, and investors.

**Strong signals:**
- Compelling narrative about the problem and solution
- Can sell the vision without overselling
- History of recruiting others (co-founders, early employees, customers)
- Persuasive but not slick

**Weak signals:**
- Can't explain their company in one sentence
- Relies on jargon instead of clarity
- Doesn't ask for what they need (the sale, the hire, the investment)
- Comes across as scripted or rehearsed rather than authentic

**Assessment questions:**
- "Explain your company to me as if I have no context." (Then: "Now explain it as if I'm an expert.")
- "Walk me through how you got your first customer."
- "How would you sell this to someone who doesn't think they have this problem?"

## Interview Techniques

### The 10-Minute Test

In 10 minutes, you should be able to determine:
1. What the company does
2. Why the founders are the right people
3. Whether users want it
4. Whether the market is big enough

If you can't, the founders either don't understand their own business or can't communicate it clearly.

### The Pivot Test

Ask: "If this approach doesn't work, what would you do?"

- Good founders have thought about adjacent opportunities
- Great founders have a clear theory about what upstream problem they're solving
- Bad founders have no answer or give up

### The Traction Test

Ask: "What metrics would convince you that this is working?"

- Good founders have specific, measurable milestones
- Great founders have milestones that indicate product-market fit
- Bad founders cite vanity metrics or can't answer

### The Speed Test

Between first and second meeting:
- Good progress = strong signal
- Same status = yellow flag
- No progress or avoidance = red flag

## Reference Check Guide

### Who to Talk To

For each founder, talk to:
1. **Former manager** — Work ethic, ability, coachability
2. **Former report** — Leadership style, integrity
3. **Peer/collaborator** — Teamwork, communication, reliability
4. **Customer** (if available) — Product-market understanding

### Key Questions

| Question | What You're Assessing |
|----------|----------------------|
| "What are they like to work with?" | Collaboration, interpersonal skills |
| "Tell me about a time they faced a setback" | Resilience, problem-solving |
| "What would they need to improve?" | Self-awareness, growth areas |
| "Would you invest your own money?" | Overall conviction |
| "How do they handle disagreement?" | Coachability, ego |
| "Are they the same person in good times and bad?" | Consistency, stability |

### Red Flags from References

- Hesitation when asked about integrity
- "They work very hard" without specifics on results
- Qualified endorsements ("well, for this role...")
- Multiple references mention the same weakness

## Scoring

### Individual Founder Scorecard

| Dimension | Weight | Score (1-5) | Evidence |
|-----------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Founder-market fit | 3x | | |
| Execution velocity | 2x | | |
| Resilience & grit | 2x | | |
| Intellectual honesty | 2x | | |
| Sales ability | 1x | | |
| Technical skill | 1x | | |
| Domain expertise | 1x | | |

**Strong founder:** Weighted score ≥ 36
**Solid founder:** Weighted score 28-35
**Concerning:** Weighted score < 28

### Founder Score Cheat Sheet

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 5 | Exceptional — one of the best you've seen |
| 4 | Strong — would actively want to work with |
| 3 | Adequate — no concerns but no excitement |
| 2 | Below average — specific concerns |
| 1 | Red flag — would not invest |

## Team Dynamics

For co-founding teams, assess these dynamics beyond individual scores:

### Co-Founder Relationships
- **How did they meet?** — Long history is better than recent matching
- **Have they worked together before?** — Previous collaboration is the best predictor
- **How do they resolve disagreements?** — Healthy conflict resolution is essential
- **Equity split** — Near-equal is ideal (50/50 with vesting). Extreme splits (80/20) signal misalignment.

### Team Composition
- **Builder + Seller** — Ideal combination. Someone who can build the product and someone who can sell it.
- **Technical depth** — Can they build the v1 without hiring?
- **Complementary skills** — Overlap is wasted equity. Gaps need a hiring plan.
- **Shared mission** — Both founders should care deeply about the problem.

### Solo Founder Assessment
Solo founders can succeed, but the bar is higher. Assess:
- **Self-awareness of gaps** — Do they know what they're missing?
- **Recruitment plan** — Concrete plan to fill gaps, not just "I'll hire"
- **Support network** — Mentors, advisors, community
- **Burnout risk** — Single point of failure is a real risk
