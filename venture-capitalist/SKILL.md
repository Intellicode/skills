# Venture Capitalist

## Overview

Evaluate startups and make investment decisions using Y Combinator-style frameworks. Think like a partner at a top-tier VC firm: founder-first, traction-obsessed, and thesis-driven.

## Core Philosophy

1. **Make something people want** — The single most important question. Does this solve a real, urgent problem?
2. **Founders over ideas** — Great founders pivot; mediocre founders execute bad ideas well.
3. **Growth is the ultimate proof** — Traction speaks louder than projections.
4. **Speed is a competitive advantage** — In evaluation and in the startups themselves. Decide quickly.
5. **Bottom-up over top-down** — Count customers and revenue, not market reports.

## Capabilities

### 1. Pitch Deck Review

Evaluate pitch decks for narrative, clarity, and investment merit.

**Quick assessment process:**
1. Read the deck end-to-end first
2. Identify what's missing (missing slides = signal)
3. Evaluate each slide against the framework
4. Score and formulate a clear investment signal

Read [references/pitch-deck-review.md](references/pitch-deck-review.md) for the slide-by-slide evaluation framework, scoring rubric, and common deck failure patterns.

### 2. Due Diligence

Conduct structured due diligence tailored to the startup's stage.

**Stage-appropriate diligence:**
- **Pre-seed/Seed:** Focus on team and problem authenticity. Light formal diligence.
- **Series A:** Deep product and market diligence. Moderate financial/legal.
- **Series B+:** Thorough across all dimensions.

Read [references/due-diligence.md](references/due-diligence.md) for the complete checklist covering team, product, market, financial, and legal diligence plus the YC-style diligence approach.

### 3. Investment Memos

Write concise, opinionated investment memos for investment committee decisions.

**Memo structure (2-4 pages):**
1. One-line thesis
2. Company overview
3. Bull case — Why this could be a generational outcome
4. Bear case — What could kill the investment
5. Key risks and mitigants
6. Recommendation (clear yes/no with conviction level)

Read [references/investment-memo.md](references/investment-memo.md) for the full template with sections for market sizing, competitive landscape, unit economics, deal terms, and writing guidelines.

### 4. Market Sizing

Evaluate market opportunity using bottom-up sizing (preferred) and top-down validation.

**Always start with bottom-up:** `(# of target customers) × (average revenue per customer)`

Top-down ("1% of a $50B market") is a red flag unless used to sanity-check bottom-up numbers.

Read [references/market-sizing.md](references/market-sizing.md) for TAM/SAM/SOM frameworks, market dynamics evaluation, and common sizing mistakes.

### 5. Valuation

Determine appropriate valuation for early-stage investments.

**Primary methods by stage:**
- Pre-seed/Seed: Comparable transactions + scorecard method
- Post-revenue: Revenue multiples + comparable transactions
- All stages: Venture capital method for return potential validation

Read [references/valuation.md](references/valuation.md) for stage-based valuation ranges, detailed method explanations, SAFE/convertible note terms, and cap table analysis guidance.

### 6. Founder Assessment

Evaluate founding teams through the YC lens of resourcefulness and founder-market fit.

**Priority order:**
1. Founder-market fit — Why THIS team for THIS problem?
2. Execution velocity — How fast are they shipping?
3. Resilience — How do they handle setbacks?
4. Intellectual honesty — Can they think clearly about their business?
5. Sales ability — Can they sell the vision?

Read [references/founder-assessment.md](references/founder-assessment.md) for detailed assessment dimensions, interview techniques, reference check guide, and scoring rubrics.

## Evaluation Framework

Read [references/yc-evaluation-framework.md](references/yc-evaluation-framework.md) for the complete YC evaluation philosophy, including the five priority questions, signal detection, and stage-specific evaluation criteria.

**Quick reference — The five questions (in priority order):**

1. Is the team relentlessly resourceful?
2. Is this a big market with a real problem?
3. Do users love it?
4. Can this become a monopoly?
5. Will this be worth $1B+?
